In this chapter, we will research and document the range of investment strategies, associated investment vehicles (which make strategies available for investment), and any other services offered by your firm.

Investment strategies

An investment strategy is simply an approach to investing in some area of the financial markets. Once an area of focus is chosen — such as the global stock market, U.S. stock market, or European bond market — an appropriate market index is selected, which makes up the universe of available investments for the strategy.

For example, a investment management firm may decide to create a global equity strategy that focuses on the global stock market. From the range of available market indices, they could choose the MSCI All Country World Index (ACWI) — a global stock market index that contains nearly 3,000 companies from around the world — as the investment universe for the strategy.

Next, an important distinction is made: whether the strategy is active or passive.

Passive investment strategies

A passive strategy aims to replicate the portfolio of investments in the appropriate market index. If the global equity strategy from the previous example was created as a passive strategy, it would hold the same companies as the ACWI, in exactly the same weights — or as closely as is practical (the smallest weighted holdings are sometimes excluded from strategies that replicate an index with a large number of total holdings).

Importantly, market indexes are theoretical: they can't be invested in directly. Creating a passive strategy is a first step in making an index available for actual investment (the second is to launch an associated investment vehicle, which we will cover shortly).

Active investment strategies

In contrast to passive strategies — which simply recreate a market index — the portfolio managers of an active strategy select a portion of investments from the index that they believe have the best long-term prospects.

Typically, an active strategy will have a set of guidelines in place: the number of holdings to be held in the strategy portfolio, a maximum size of any single holding as a percentage of the total portfolio, requirements for individual holdings (a minimum company size for stocks, or credit rating for bonds), and so on.

Working within the guidelines, portfolio managers use an investment process to select investments from the index to hold in the strategy portfolio (holdings may vary over time, as conditions change). The guidelines and investment process usually incorporates principles from the managing firm's overall investment philosophy.

Performance benchmarks

For both active and passive strategies, the market index is used as a benchmark to periodically compare performance against: passive strategies attempt to match the index performance as closely as possible, whereas active strategies attempt to beat it.

Investment vehicles

Like a market index, an investment strategy is hypothetical, and can't be invested in directly. To allow investment, there must be an associated investment vehicle available, which gives investors something tangible to buy and sell. The investment vehicle allows an investment strategy to be "put into practice" — stocks, bonds, or other securities are selected according to an associated strategy, held within a vehicle, and actual investment results are delivered to those who invest in the vehicle.

Multiple investment vehicles can be associated with a single strategy, with each vehicle holding the same composition of underlying investments (although there may be some variation due to customised portfolios for specific investors or restrictions that apply to some types of vehicle). When changes are made to holdings within the strategy portfolio, these are usually applied across all investment vehicles associated with the strategy.

For our purposes, we will differentiate between two major types of investment vehicle: pooled investment funds and separately managed accounts (SMAs).

Pooled investment funds

A pooled investment fund is an investment vehicle in which money is pooled together from multiple investors to hold underlying investments (stocks, bonds, and so on), chosen by the fund's investment manager, in accordance with an associated investment strategy. Investors can purchase units of the pooled fund to access the associated strategy. Examples of pooled funds include mutual funds and exchange traded funds (ETFs).

Pooled investment funds can be launched for investors within a single country (such as the United States), and also — if they are built with a UCITS structure — can be marketed and sold in multiple countries, most commonly in the European Union.

To launch UCITS funds, an investment manager must first set up a fund company that is domiciled in a country within the European Union (usually Luxembourg or Ireland, for infrastructure and tax reasons). Once in place, UCITS funds can be launched from within this corporate entity, with each fund known as a sub-fund of the fund company.

Share classes

Pooled funds offer options to investors through their share classes. Investors choose a share class from those made available by a pooled fund's investment manager when making an investment. While all investors in a pooled fund — no matter which share class they choose — own the same underlying pool of investments, each share class will offer some form of customisation (a full explanation of an investment manager's share classes are provided in regulatory documents).

In the United States, the only distinction between mutual fund share classes is the amount of fees and expenses charged to investors, whereas UCITS share classes offer more options to investors — and also determine their availability by country.

As mentioned previously, a UCITS sub-fund must be registered and approved by the regulator in the domiciled country of the fund company. Then — to make a sub-fund available for investment — at least one share class must be created. Each share class must also be registered and approved in the domiciled country, where it can then be sold. From this point, the UCITS framework allows approved share classes to be freely registered for sale in any other country within the European Union (called passporting). UCITS funds and share classes can also be registered for sale in countries outside the European Union, but the normal application process must be followed (i.e., no passporting is allowed).

As the availability of UCITS funds to investors in a specific country is determined at the share class level, a document is usually maintained by the investment manager's operations team that records the countries in which each share class is registered (a very helpful resource to ensure UCITS fund-related marketing activity is targeted correctly).

The options that UCITS share classes can offer to investors include — in addition to various fee levels — a choice for (1) how dividends or interest payments received from investments in the fund are distributed to investors, (2) the currency of the share class net-asset-value (NAV), and (3) whether the investment manager attempts to minimise the impact of foreign exchange movements on investment returns, where relevant.

Each UCITS share class typically incorporates a combination of options, but it's unlikely that an investment manager will offer all possible combinations of share class options, and there are no rules that dictate if any UCITS share classes must be made available as a minimum. Therefore, we can assume the decision of which share classes to offer is left to investment managers, who will likely create them in expectation of client demand (for example, in popular currencies) or when they have been specifically requested (a potential client may request a new share class with a specific set of options). Conversely — as share classes have ongoing running costs — those with low interest or a low amount of invested capital may be closed by the investment manager. Some investment managers offer 20+ share classes per UCITS fund, so the launching and closing process can happen regularly.

Share class currencies and currency hedging

We can also look at Lewisham's available share classes to help us understand how currency and hedging options can work in practice. Let's use the Lewisham US Equity UCITS Fund, which holds underlying investments — the stocks of US companies — that are priced in US Dollars (USD). The fund's base currency is also set to USD (if all a sub-fund's underlying investments are within a single country, the sub-fund's base currency will typically match the currency of the underlying investments).

The fund's net asset value (NAV) is calculated once per day, in the base currency, by dividing the total value of the investments held in the fund by the number of fund units. Dividing holdings valued at $100m by 10 million fund units would give a daily NAV of $10.00: investors can purchase or sell a unit of the fund at this price on that day. The NAV may rise or fall the next day; if the fund's investments increase in value at $102 million, and the number of units stay the same, the NAV would become $10.20 (a return of +2.00%).

The first share class launched for the Lewisham US Equity UCITS Fund was denominated in USD, the same as the fund's base currency. However, UCITS funds are aimed primarily at investors in the European Union, who will have a different local currency — Euro (EUR), Pound Sterling (GBP), Swiss Franc (CHF), and so on. This means that at some point, a currency exchange must occur between the USD share class and the investor's local currency (possibly made by a broker that handled the transaction, who may set a disadvantageous rate for the investor). For example, a UK-based investor's holdings in the Lewisham US Equity UCITS Fund's USD share class must at some point be converted to GBP.

Investment managers can make it more convenient for investors by offering share classes priced in their local currency (and presumably, they also use a better exchange rate for investors than brokers). Investment performance is also calculated in the relevant currency. Lewisham offer a share class for the US Equity UCITS Fund for UK investors, with its NAV priced in GBP. Roughly speaking, if the USD NAV is $10.00, and the USD/GBP exchange rate is 0.82, the GBP NAV would be roughly £8.20.

To further complicate matters, currency exchange rates change over time. Therefore, the investment performance of the GBP share class will deviate from the USD share class — even though they share the same underlying fund investments. For example, if the investments held by the Lewisham US Equity UCITS Fund rise in value by 2.00%, the USD NAV would rise by the same amount — as the investments are also priced in USD. However, performance of the GBP share class is also affected by changes in the USD/GBP exchange rate. If, during the same period, the USD/GBP rate moved from 0.82 to 0.81 (the GBP weakens in value by 1.22% against the USD) investors in the GBP share class investors would receive a lower return of around 0.76%.

As shown in figure 8, the return of a share class can be split into two components: (1) fund performance in the base currency (e.g., USD), and (2) the change in value of the share class currency versus the fund's base currency, if they are different (e.g., USD/GBP).

For investors who are uncomfortable with the currency risk, some investment managers offer currency hedged share classes, in which the fund managers attempt to remove the impact of exchange rate changes on the share class return — usually via currency forward contracts. Investors buy and sell the share class in their local currency, but only receive the fund return component in the base currency (essentially, the return of the underlying investments). Note, however, that currency hedging is imperfect — it can't remove 100% of the currency risk — so investment performance of a hedged share class will still be slightly affected by exchange rate changes.

Lewisham offer a GBP-hedged share class to UK-based investors for the Lewisham US Equity UCITS Fund which, although priced in GBP, attempts to remove the effect of changes between USD/GBP, so that the share class return matches the return of the underlying investments in their base currency (as closely as possible).

Note that more complex examples exist than those we've covered — for example, funds can hold investments priced in multiple currencies, other types of currency hedging are attempted, and so on — but they are out of scope for this guide.

Separately managed accounts (SMAs)

Investors with enough money to invest (an amount determined by each individual investment manager) can open their own account directly with an investment manager, who buys and sells underlying investments — according to the associated investment strategy — within the account.

Separately managed accounts have some potential advantages when compared to pooled investment funds:

  • Direct ownership of underlying investments. With pooled investment funds, investors own a share of the investment vehicle, which in turn owns the underlying investments. With an SMA, the investor directly owns the underlying securities.
  • Customisation. Unlike pooled investment funds, which can't be customised beyond the existing share class options, the associated strategy for a separately managed account can be individually customised for each account.
  • Tax benefits. As the underlying investments are directly owned by the investor, there may be potential tax benefits via tax-loss harvesting strategies
  • Transparency. More comprehensive and regular reporting for all underlying investments in the portfolio is made available.
  • Negotiable fees. Fees charged by the investment manager can be negotiated by the investor, and may sometimes be lower than actively managed mutual funds.

Fees

The primary source of revenue for investment managers comes from the fees they charge for managing investment vehicles. These are usually specified as a percentage of assets and periodically taken out from the capital within the vehicle (an additional performance fee is sometimes charged on profits but are not as common).

Fees can vary quite widely across vehicles, strategies, and share classes, but generally, vehicles with lower fees come with a higher investment minimum requirement. Because fees are taken out of the vehicle's capital, and not billed separately to investors, investment performance of the vehicle is reduced by the amount of the fee each time it is charged.
