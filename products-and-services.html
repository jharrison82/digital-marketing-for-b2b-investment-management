In this chapter, we will research and document the range of investment strategies, associated investment vehicles (which make strategies available for investment), and any other services offered by your firm.

Investment strategies

An investment strategy is simply an approach to investing in some area of the financial markets. Once an area of focus is chosen — such as the global stock market, U.S. stock market, or European bond market — an appropriate market index is selected, which makes up the universe of available investments for the strategy.

For example, a investment management firm may decide to create a global equity strategy, that focused on the global stock market. From the range of available market indices, they could choose the MSCI All Country World Index (ACWI) — a global stock market index that contains nearly 3,000 companies from around the world — as the investment universe for the strategy.

The five largest stock holdings in the ACWI, which is based on the company's market capitalisation, are shown in figure 1.

Next, an important distinction is made: whether the strategy is active or passive.

Passive investment strategies

A passive strategy aims to replicate the portfolio of investments in the appropriate market index. If the global equity strategy from the previous example was created as a passive strategy, it would hold the same companies as the ACWI in exactly the same weights — or as closely as is practical (for logistical reasons, the smallest weighted holdings are sometimes excluded from strategies that replicate an index with a large number of total holdings).

Importantly, market indexes are theoretical: they can't be invested in directly. Creating a passive strategy is a first step in making an index available for actual investment (the second is to launch an associated investment vehicle, which we will cover shortly).

Active investment strategies

In contrast to passive strategies — which simply recreate a market index — the portfolio managers of an active strategy select a portion of investments from the index that they believe have the best long-term prospects.

Typically, an active strategy will have a set of guidelines in place: the number of holdings to be held in the strategy portfolio, a maximum size of any single holding as a percentage of the total portfolio, requirements for individual holdings (a minimum company size for stocks, or credit rating for bonds), and so on.

Working within the guidelines, portfolio managers use an investment process to select investments from the index to hold in the strategy portfolio (holdings may vary over time, as conditions change). The guidelines and investment process usually incorporates principles from the managing firm's overall investment philosophy.

Performance benchmarks

For both active and passive strategies, the market index is used as a benchmark to periodically compare performance against: passive strategies attempt to match the index performance as closely as possible, whereas active strategies attempt to beat it.

Investment vehicles

Like a market index, an investment strategy is hypothetical, and can't be invested in directly. To allow investment, there must be an associated investment vehicle available, which gives investors something tangible to buy and sell. The investment vehicle allows an investment strategy to be "put into practice — stocks, bonds, or other securities are selected according to an associated strategy, held within a vehicle, and actual investment results are delivered to those who invest in the vehicle.

Multiple investment vehicles can be associated with a single strategy, with each vehicle made up of the same composition of underlying investments (although there may be some variations due to customised portfolios for specific investors or restrictions that apply to some types of vehicle). When changes are made to holdings within the strategy portfolio, these are usually applied across all investment vehicles associated with the strategy.

For our purposes, we will differentiate between two major types of investment vehicle: separately managed accounts (SMAs) and pooled investment funds.

Pooled investment funds

A pooled investment fund is an investment vehicle in which money is pooled together from multiple investors to hold underlying investments (stocks, bonds, and so on) chosen by the fund's investment manager in accordance with an associated investment strategy. Investors can purchase units of the pooled fund to access the associated strategy. Examples of pooled funds include mutual funds and exchange traded funds (ETFs).

Pooled investment funds can be launched for investors within a single country (such as the United States), and also — if they are built with a UCITS structure — can be marketed and sold in multiple countries.

To launch UCITS funds, an investment manager must set up a fund company domiciled in a country within the European Union (usually Luxembourg or Ireland, for infrastructure and tax reasons). Once in place, UCITS funds can be launched from within this corporate entity, with each fund known as a sub-fund of the fund company.

Share classes

Pooled funds offer options to investors through their share classes. Investors choose a share class from those made available by a pooled fund's investment manager when making an investment. While all investors in a pooled fund — no matter which share class they choose — own the same underlying pool of investments, each share class will offer some form of customisation. Explanations of an investment manager's available share classes are provided in their regulatory documents.

In the United States, the only distinction between mutual fund share classes is the amount of fees and expenses charged to investors, whereas UCITS share classes offer more options to investors — and also determine their availability by country.

A UCITS sub-fund must be registered and approved by the regulator in the domiciled country of the fund company. Then — to make a sub-fund available for investment — at least one share class must be created. Each share class must also be registered and approved in the domiciled country, where it can then be sold. From this point, the UCITS framework allows approved share classes to be freely registered for sale in any other country within the European Union (called "passporting"). UCITS funds and share classes can be registered for sale in countries outside the European Union, but the normal application process must be followed (i.e., no passporting is allowed).

As the availability of UCITS funds to investors in a specific country is determined at the share class level, a document is usually maintained by the investment manager's operations team that records the countries in which each share class is registered (a very helpful resource to ensure UCITS fund-related marketing activity is targeted correctly).

UCITS share classes can offer multiple options to investors. In addition to different fee levels, they also offer a choice for (1) how dividends or interest payments received from investments in the fund are distributed to investors, (2) the currency of the share class net-asset-value (NAV), and (3) whether the investment manager attempts to minimise the impact of foreign exchange movements on investment returns, where relevant.

Each share class will typically incorporate a combination of options, but it's unlikely that an investment manager will offer all possible combinations of share class options, and there are no rules that dictate if any UCITS share classes must be made available as a minimum. Therefore, we can assume this decision is left to investment managers, who will likely create share classes in expectation of client demand (for example, in popular currencies) or when they have been specifically requested (a potential client may request a new share class with a specific set of options). Conversely – as share classes have ongoing running costs – those with low interest or a low amount of invested capital may be closed by the investment manager. Some investment managers offer 20+ share classes per UCITS fund, so the launching and closing process can happen regularly.

The share class options offered by Lewisham — which are the same as those of most investment managers — attempt to meet a range of investor needs:

An International Securities Identification Number, more commonly known as an ISIN, is shown for each share class. As well as serving as the unique identifier for a share class – like a ticker for a stock or US mutual fund – the ISIN is often used to search for share classes in search engines or on investment manager websites, instead of the name (which can sometimes be quite long).

Something that I recently learned is that professional investors can invest in any UCITS share class they wish, even if it is not registered in their country (and their country is outside the European Union). Despite this, share classes which aren’t registered in a specific country still cannot be marketed to investors who are in that country (such as being displayed on a website), even if they identify themselves as professional investors. This means that you may see countries outside those that a share class is registered in for breakdowns of AUM by investor country, but it won’t affect marketing efforts.

Separately managed accounts (SMAs)

Investors with enough money to invest (an amount determined by each individual investment manager) can open their own account directly with an investment manager, who buys and sells underlying investments – according to the associated investment strategy – within the account. SMAs have some potential advantages to pooled investment funds: (1) the investor directly owns the underlying securities instead of just a share of the vehicle, (2) the investment strategy can be individually customised for each account, (3) there may be potential tax benefits for investors, (4) more detailed and regular reporting is available, and (5) fees charged by the investment manager are usually lower.

Fees

The primary source of revenue for investment managers comes from the fees they charge for managing investment vehicles. These are usually specified as a percentage of assets and periodically taken out from the capital within the vehicle (an additional performance fee is sometimes charged on profits but are not as common).

Fees can vary quite widely across vehicles, strategies, and share classes, but generally, vehicles with lower fees come with a higher investment minimum requirement. Because fees are taken out of the vehicle’s capital, and not billed separately to investors, investment performance of the vehicle is reduced by the amount of the fee each time it is charged.
