In this chapter, we will define the target audience for your firm.

Target organisations

We will begin by looking at the types of organisations that B2B investment managent firms typically work with: institutions, institutional investment consultants & OCIOs, retail advisors, and investment platforms.

Institutions

An institution is an entity that invests on behalf of others, or itself, such as a pension fund (investing contributions of plan participants), insurance firm (investing premiums paid by customers), or a sovereign wealth fund (investing oil revenues).

Institutions need to invest very large sums of money, and so usually have some form of internal investment committee or board of trustees that decides overall investment strategy, allocates the pool of money across asset classes, and hires investment managers to manage specific portions of the institution's portfolio.

A more comprehensive list of institutions includes government and corporate pension funds, family offices, endowments, foundations, central banks, sovereign wealth funds, and insurance firms. The term also includes other organisations — such as hedge funds, mutual funds, and REITs — which aren't typical clients of B2B investment managers.

Institutional investment consultants & OCIOs

Institutions without investment expertise can outsource the work to an institutional investment consultant or a provider of outsourced chief investment officer (OCIO) services.

Both consultants and OCIOs can help institutions with asset allocation and manager selection. However, unlike consultants, OCIOs can typically make additional discretionary decisions on the institution's behalf — allowing the institution to essentially outsource its entire investment team.

Investment consultants maintain databases of investment managers and create shortlists for recommendations to their institutional clients.

Retail advisors

Retail advisors provide various forms of financial advice to retail investors as either an independent firm or part of a larger organisation — such as the wealth management department (or subsidiary) of a bank.

The specific services they provide and the level of wealth of their clients can vary, but any organisation that provides financial advice to retail investors — including wealth managers, private banks, financial advisors, investment advisors, and financial planners — are included here.

Each firm will maintain an internal shortlist of products (usually pooled investment funds) for use with their retail clients.

Investment platforms

An investment platform allows groups of retail investors or advisors to buy and sell a range of investment products — offered by a variety of investment managers — in one place.

Platforms are used by financial advisors to manage their clients' portfolios, and by retail investors to manage personal investments (sometimes called fund supermarkets).

Client groups

Each type of target organisation can be placed within either an institutional or intermediary client group (or channel), as shown in figure 1 (which also shows how each organisation generally interacts with other investors).

Of the two groups, institutional clients are considered to be the most sophisticated, tend to already know what they want when speaking with investment managers, take more time to make decisions, and stick with their investments for much longer.

For example, a pension fund may allocate $250 million of their portfolio to the US stock market, and either search for an investment manager to manage the allocation according to an investment mandate, or hire a consultant to recommend one. In either case, lengthy due diligence will be conducted on each investment manager's expertise, personnel, investment process, and risk management practices — not just the performance of a strategy.

With intermediary clients, investment managers focus on getting their pooled funds added to shortlists and investment platforms, which can significantly increase inflows into those products. As they ultimately serve retail clients, who tend to sell quickly when markets drop, money invested by intermediaries is considered less "sticky" (i.e., much more likely to be sold) than investments from institutional clients.

Target individuals

A B2B investment manager works with a variety of individuals employed by target organisations, but the sales team are only interested in those who can allocate money, either directly or indirectly, into your firm's products.

Rather than focusing on the seemingly endless array of industry classifications or job titles, we can identity these key individuals based on their job function (i.e., what they do), which means the resulting list, shown in figure 2, can apply globally.

Advice givers are client-facing individuals who provide investment advice: the financial advisors, wealth managers, or private bankers at retail advisors (who advise retail investors), and field consultants at institutional investment consultants (who advise institutions). These individuals are responsible for allocating client money to investment products.

Investment advice usually contains recommendations from pre-approved shortlists compiled by gatekeepers who perform manager research or fund selection, who are also responsible for adding pooled funds and creating shortists on retail investment platforms. Gatekeepers can make a manager, fund or strategy available to large groups of advisors, consultants, and retail investors — hence the name.

Individuals who manage investment portfolios are the portfolio managers and chief investment officers (CIOs) who devise investment strategies, make asset allocation decisions, and construct portfolios of investments for institutions.

Professional investors tend to make decisions in groups, rather than as individuals, as they can have a significant impact on large numbers of clients or beneficiaries.

Sales team structure

Next, we will look how our firm organises sales coverage to cover the various client groups and target organisations — which varies across investment managers.

Although it depends on a firm's size, B2B investment managers usually have multiple sales teams, or individual salespeople, which are each assigned coverage of a client group, target organisation, or geographic area (or various combinations). Geographic areas include individual countries (France, Spain, or Germany), a group of countries (the Nordics, DACH, or Benelux), or parts of a country (a state or region within the United States).

Some investment managers also have a dedicated team for their most important clients identified at an account (or firm) level, known as strategic accounts.

To help fill any gaps in sales coverage, client service or sales support teams can sometimes cover relationships with clients or prospects who are located in a geographic area that isn't covered by existing sales teams or salespeople.

Lewisham's sales team structure

The sales team structure of Lewisham Asset Management (our fictional investment management firm) is detailed in figure 2.

Lewisham divides the sales team into United States and International teams (the international team covers everywhere else globally). Note that coverage is determined by the location of each individual contact and not the location of the organisation they work for (which could be global).

Within the United States, three separate teams each cover intermediaries, institutions, investment consultants & OCIOs. Sales directors within the intermediary team are each assigned a region to covser, and they work "on the road" (they are known as external, or outside, salespeople). The more junior internal salespeople sit in the New York City office and cover smaller clients, contact new leads by phone and emaiol, try and win new contacts, and provide admin and support the external sales people.

Internationally, a single team covers both intermediary and institutional relationships, with individual salespeople covering specific countries or regions; another team covers consultant relationships outside the United States. One salesperson within the international team has been assigned to cover intermediary and institutional relationships with individuals in regions that don’t have sales coverage – such as Asia or the Middle East.
